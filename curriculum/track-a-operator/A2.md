# Documents & data: from a wall of text (or a messy sheet) to the answer you need

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
The report you have to read, the spreadsheet you have to clean, the contract you have to skim for one clause — this is where hours quietly disappear in {{industry}}. Today Claude reads, extracts, and reformats it for you, so you spend your time *deciding*, not *digging* — and every document task that lands on your plate is a step toward **{{deliverable}}**.

## 2. Concept
Claude is genuinely strong at document and data work: summarizing long material, extracting specific fields, reformatting between shapes (prose ↔ table ↔ bullets), and spotting patterns or outliers. You can paste text directly, or **attach files** (PDFs, docs, spreadsheets, images of tables) right in the chat and ask questions about them.

**Framework — SHAPE → FLAG → CITE → VERIFY (the document analysis standard):**

1. **SHAPE:** Tell Claude the exact output format before it reads anything — *"a 5-column table with these exact headers"* beats *"summarize this."* A defined shape turns analysis into something you can immediately use.
2. **FLAG:** Instruct it to surface every ambiguous item as a list, not silently categorize them. *"If a row is unclear, list it separately"* is the line that saves you from invisible errors.
3. **CITE:** For every number or extracted claim, require the source — *"tell me which row/line this came from."* That's what lets you spot-check in seconds instead of re-reading the whole document.
4. **VERIFY:** Before you act on any result, check the flagged items and at least one figure against the source. This takes two minutes and is the difference between fast and reckless.

Claude is a fast, tireless analyst's assistant; it is *not* a calculator or a system of record, so you keep the final check on anything numeric.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Grab a real document or sheet from {{industry}} that you need to work through (or paste a chunk of one). Run:

```
I've attached/pasted [WHAT IT IS — e.g. "a client's monthly expense CSV"].
Do this:
1. [THE CORE JOB — e.g. "categorize each expense and total by category"].
2. Output as [EXACT SHAPE — e.g. "a table: category · total · # of items"].
3. Flag anything you're unsure about in a separate "Needs review" list, and
   for each number, tell me which row/line it came from.
Don't guess silently — if a value is ambiguous, list it instead of forcing a category.
```

Read the result, then spot-check the flagged items and one or two numbers against the source. That verify step takes a minute and is the difference between fast and reckless.

> **Privacy note:** this is real client/business data — only attach what you're authorized to process, and prefer redacted or anonymized files (e.g. strip names/account numbers) when the analysis doesn't need them.

### 🎁 Keepable asset — the "Analyze & Verify" block
```
I've attached/pasted [WHAT].
1. [the analysis job]
2. Output as [exact table/format].
3. Put anything ambiguous in a "Needs review" list; cite the source row/line for each number.
Never force a guess silently — surface it instead.
(Then I check the flagged items and spot-verify ≥1 number before acting.)
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **Asking to "summarize"** with no shape — you get prose when you needed a table.
- **Trusting totals blindly.** Claude can miscount or mis-add on big/ugly data. Always have it cite sources and spot-check.
- **Dumping a 200-page PDF and asking everything at once.** Ask focused questions; it's more accurate per-question.
- **Forgetting the "flag uncertainty" line** — without it, ambiguous rows get silently (and sometimes wrongly) categorized.

### 🔎 Honest limit
Claude is a language model, not a spreadsheet engine — on large or messy datasets it can make arithmetic or counting errors and state them confidently. For anything that must foot to the penny (invoices, financials, payroll), use it to *structure and draft*, then verify totals in your real tools. The "cite the source row" habit is your safety net.

### 📊 Case study — why "cite the source row" is non-negotiable
**Illustrative of a documented model limitation.** Language models predict text; they don't run a spreadsheet engine, so on long or messy data they can sum, count, or transcribe a figure *confidently wrong*. This isn't a Claude-specific flaw — it's inherent to how LLMs work, which is why Anthropic publishes explicit [hallucination-reduction guidance](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/test-and-evaluate/strengthen-guardrails/reduce-hallucinations) (allow "I don't know," ground answers in provided documents, ask for citations). The CITE step turns this from a hidden risk into a 30-second spot-check: when every number points back to a source row, verifying ten figures takes less time than re-reading one page.
