# Research & synthesis: a sourced briefing in minutes, not an afternoon

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
"Go find out about X and summarize it" is a half-day rabbit hole — whether you're scanning the market in {{industry}} or gathering background for **{{deliverable}}**. Done right, Claude turns it into a structured, *sourced* briefing in minutes — and, just as importantly, teaches you to spot when it's bluffing.

## 2. Concept
Research is two jobs: **gathering** and **synthesizing**. Claude is excellent at synthesis — taking material and organizing it into themes, comparisons, and "so what" conclusions. For *gathering* current facts, it depends on the tool: in Claude apps with **web search** enabled, it can pull live sources and link them; the model on its own (no search) answers from training and **does not know recent events**.

**Framework — SCOPE → SOURCE → MARK → CHECK (the sourced research standard):**

1. **SCOPE:** Define the question tightly and name the output shape before you start — *"a comparison table of these 3 competitors on price, audience, and key feature"* beats an open-ended question. Scope also decides your mode: live web search (for current facts) vs. synthesize-only-from-pasted-material (for documents you control).
2. **SOURCE:** Require a citation for every claim. In web-search mode: a link. In paste mode: quote the sentence it came from. A claim without a source is an opinion, not research.
3. **MARK:** Force all uncertain or unsourceable items to be explicitly labeled — *"mark it 'unverified' rather than guessing."* This single instruction separates trustworthy AI research from confidently-stated fiction.
4. **CHECK:** Open at least two sources (or check two claims against your pasted material) before acting on the result. If a link is dead or doesn't say what was claimed, that's the lesson — call it out and re-run.

The one thing to know about research with language models: they can **invent confident, real-looking sources that don't exist.** MARK and CHECK are not optional.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Pick a real research question from your work in {{industry}}. Choose your mode and run it:

```
RESEARCH TASK: [your question — e.g. "compare these 3 competitors on price, audience, and key feature"]
MODE: [ "Use web search and give a source link for every claim"
        OR  "Use ONLY the material I pasted below: [PASTE]" ]
OUTPUT: [shape — e.g. "a comparison table, then a 3-bullet 'so what' synthesis"]
RULES: If a fact is uncertain or unsourced, mark it "unverified" — do NOT guess.
       Separate what's well-supported from what's speculative.
```

Then **click at least two sources** (or check two claims against your pasted material). If a link is broken or doesn't say what was claimed, that's the lesson — call it out and re-run.

> **Privacy note:** for competitive/market scans on public info you're fine; don't paste confidential client material into a web-search query you don't control.

## 5. Check

- You **separated gathering from synthesis** (web-search-with-sources OR synthesize-only-from-pasted).
- Every claim is **sourced or marked "unverified"** — nothing silently guessed.
- You **opened at least two sources to confirm** them.

```
       separate well-supported from speculative.

```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **Not asking for sources.** Unsourced research from an AI is a rumor, not research.
- **Assuming it browsed.** Without web search on, it's answering from memory and may be out of date.
- **Trusting links blindly.** Open a couple — occasionally a link is real but doesn't actually support the claim.
- **Mixing gathering and synthesis** in one vague ask, so you can't tell fact from inference.

### 🔎 Honest limit
This is the area to be most careful: language models can **fabricate citations, statistics, and quotes that look completely real.** Without web search, Claude also has a knowledge cutoff and won't know recent developments. Never publish or decide on a sourced claim you haven't personally verified — treat Claude as a brilliant research assistant whose footnotes you always spot-check.
