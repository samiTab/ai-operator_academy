# Your chief of staff: triage the inbox, plan the day, draft the replies

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
A great chief of staff doesn't do your job — they clear the runway so you can. Today Claude becomes that person for your daily admin in {{industry}}: the email triage, the "what should I focus on," the first-draft replies that usually steal your morning — and the daily habit that builds toward **{{deliverable}}**.

## 2. Concept
A chief of staff is the operator you hand the daily churn to: *summarize this, prioritize that, draft a reply, prep me for this meeting.* Claude does all four well because they're text-in, text-out judgment tasks. The unlock beyond F1/F2 is **giving it your operating context once** — your priorities, your voice, your constraints — so its help is *yours*, not generic.

**Framework — CONTEXT → BATCH → DRAFT → DECIDE (the daily chief-of-staff loop):**

1. **CONTEXT:** Load Claude with your operating reality before anything else — your role, this week's priorities, and your communication voice. One well-written context block makes every output in the session *yours*, not generic.
2. **BATCH:** Queue multiple tasks at once — don't process one email at a time. A triage table of five emails takes the same effort as asking about one, and the comparison surfaces patterns you'd miss alone.
3. **DRAFT:** Let Claude propose the priority order, triage, and first-draft replies. Your job isn't to write from zero — it's to judge, edit, and approve.
4. **DECIDE:** You keep every final call. Claude proposes; you dispose. Send button, signature, and judgment stay with you.

This loop is what turns Claude from a one-off tool into a daily operator. The discipline is running it every morning before your first reply.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Use a real (lightly redacted) slice of your day in {{industry}}. Run this:

```
You're my chief of staff. My operating context:
- Role/business: [e.g. "owner, 8-person HVAC company"]
- My current priorities: [1–3 things that matter most this week]
- My email voice: [1 line — e.g. "direct, friendly, no corporate fluff"]

Here are this morning's messages:
[PASTE 4–6 emails or messages — redact anything sensitive]

Do three things:
1. A triage table: sender · what they want · urgency (H/M/L) · suggested action.
2. Draft replies for the High-urgency ones, in my voice.
3. My top 3 priorities for today, each with a one-line why.
```

Read the triage, edit a draft, and pick your three. You've just run your morning in a couple of minutes.

> **Privacy note:** redact client contact details, account numbers, and anything sensitive before pasting; the triage logic works fine on "Customer A re: overdue invoice."


### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **No operating context.** Without your priorities, "urgent" is its guess, not yours.
- **One email at a time.** Batch them — the triage table is where the time savings live.
- **Letting it auto-decide.** It proposes; you dispose. Keep the send button.
- **Skipping the voice line.** Otherwise replies sound like a stranger wrote them.

### 🔎 Honest limit
Claude only sees what you paste — it doesn't know that "Customer A" is actually your biggest account unless you tell it, and it can misjudge urgency without that context. It also can't send email or check your calendar on its own yet (that's MCP/connectors, Track B). Treat its priority order as a smart suggestion you adjust, not gospel.
