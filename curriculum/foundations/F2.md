# The four-part prompt that gets it right the first time

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
The gap between "AI is overhyped" and "AI saves me a day a week" is almost never the AI — it's the brief. Today you learn a repeatable four-part structure that turns vague requests into output you can actually use, on the first or second try.

## 2. Concept
Good prompting isn't magic words; it's clear delegation. Four ingredients do 90% of the work: **Context** (the situation and who it's for), **Task** (the specific thing to produce), **Format** (how the output should be shaped), and **Examples or style** (what "good" looks like to you). Think of it like a creative brief for a freelancer — give all four and you rarely get junk back.

The fifth move that separates pros from beginners is **iteration**: your first prompt is a starting point, not a one-shot. "Tighten the second paragraph, drop the jargon, give me two subject-line options" is a 5-second follow-up that's faster than fixing it yourself. Claude keeps the whole conversation in mind, so you steer rather than restart.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Pick something you actually need written or analyzed this week. Use the four-part structure:

```
CONTEXT: [Who you are, who the output is for, the situation.
  e.g. "I run social for a boutique marketing agency; this is for a client in fitness."]
TASK: [The specific thing to produce. e.g. "Write 3 Instagram captions for their new app launch."]
FORMAT: [Shape it. e.g. "Each: 2 short lines + 1 CTA + 3 hashtags. Number them 1–3."]
STYLE/EXAMPLES: [What good looks like. Paste a sample you liked, or describe the voice:
  "energetic but not salesy, like Nike not late-night infomercial."]
```

Send it, read the output, then **iterate once**: change one thing and ask for the fix. Notice the second version is noticeably closer to publish-ready.

> **Privacy note:** you can give Claude your brand voice and public details freely; don't paste a client's confidential data or credentials.

## 5. Check
Done well = (1) your prompt clearly contains **all four parts** (context, task, format, style/examples); (2) the output **matches the format you specified**; (3) you ran **at least one iteration** to improve it. Auto-checked with specific feedback.

## 6. Apply to YOUR work
Take the task you most often re-explain to people and write its four-part brief once. Ask Claude: *"Turn this into a reusable prompt template with [BRACKETS] for the parts that change each time."* Keep that template — you'll formalize these into your personal library in module A4.

## 7. Retain
**Quick check:** Name the four ingredients of a strong prompt. *(Context, Task, Format, Style/Examples — plus iterate.)* — ⭐ Nice. You now have the single most transferable skill in the course.

## 8. ROI beat
A clear four-part brief typically gets you a usable result in 1–2 tries instead of 5–6 rounds of "no, not like that." On a task you do a few times a week, that's commonly **1–2 hours back weekly** just from not fighting with vague prompts.

---

### 🎁 Keepable asset — the C-T-F-S prompt scaffold
Pin this where you write prompts:

```
CONTEXT:  who I am · who it's for · the situation
TASK:     the one specific thing to produce
FORMAT:   structure / length / sections / how to label it
STYLE:    a sample I like, or 1 line describing the voice
(then iterate: "change X, keep Y" — never restart from scratch)
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **Burying the task** in a paragraph so Claude isn't sure what the actual deliverable is. Put it on its own line.
- **Skipping Format.** Without it you get prose when you wanted a table, or 500 words when you wanted 80.
- **Describing style abstractly** ("make it good"). Paste an example — showing beats telling.
- **Restarting instead of iterating.** Steer the draft you have; it's faster and keeps the good parts.

### 🔎 Honest limit
A great prompt can't fix missing facts. If the truth lives in your head or your files (this quarter's numbers, a client's actual preferences), Claude will produce confident, well-formatted output that's *wrong* — give it the real inputs, and sanity-check specifics before anything goes out the door.
