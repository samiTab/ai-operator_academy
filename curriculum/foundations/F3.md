# Your first real win: kill one painful task in the next 10 minutes

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
Enough theory. You named a task you'd love to hand off when you signed up — let's actually do it, right now, together. In about ten minutes you'll have a real result *and* a reusable prompt you keep.

## 2. Concept
You already have the two skills you need: the teammate mindset (F1) and the four-part brief (F2). This lesson just points them at *your* painful task. The trick to a clean first win is to pick something **bounded and text-shaped** — a thing where the input and the "done" are both clear (a draft, a summary, a reply, a structured list). We're not automating it yet; we're proving it works once, by hand, so you believe it.

## 3. Demo
Here's a real one. **Maya, an independent strategy consultant**, dreads turning a 60-minute client call into a tidy follow-up: recap, decisions, action items, next steps. It eats ~40 minutes after every call.

3. Claude returns a structured, on-tone email in ~20 seconds. ![demo](screenshot: a polished follow-up email with the four labeled sections)
4. She tweaks one action-item owner and sends it. **40 minutes → ~6.** ![demo](screenshot: the finalized email with one edited line, ready to send)

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Pull up the real task you named at enrollment (or the most painful text-shaped task on your plate today). Gather the raw input. Fill this in:

```
CONTEXT: I'm a [YOUR ROLE] at [YOUR BUSINESS/CONTEXT]. This output is for [WHO].
TASK: Turn the raw input below into [THE FINISHED THING YOU NEED].
RAW INPUT:
[PASTE your messy notes / source material / the request you're responding to]
FORMAT: [sections, length, anything that must appear — e.g. "4 labeled sections, under 200 words"]
STYLE: [your voice in one line, or paste a past example you were happy with]
If a key detail is missing, ask me before guessing. Then produce it.
```

Run it. Iterate once if a section misses. Stop when you have something you'd genuinely use. That's the win.

> **Privacy note:** you can paste your own working notes; redact client names or sensitive figures if you'd rather — describe them generically (e.g. "Client A") and the output still works.

## 5. Check
Done well = (1) you used a **real task with real input**, not a hypothetical; (2) Claude returned a **usable finished artifact** in the format you asked for; (3) you got there in **≤2 iterations**. Auto-checked with specific feedback.

## 6. Apply to YOUR work
Do it a **second time** on another instance of the same task (another call, another email, another report). The second run should feel almost instant — that *repeatability* is the signal that this task is a prime candidate for the automation tracks later. Note roughly how long it used to take vs. now; you'll feed that number into your ROI tally.

## 7. Retain
**Quick check:** Why prove a task by hand once before automating it? *(To confirm the input→output works and is repeatable, so you automate something that actually delivers — not a guess.)* — 🔥🔥 Two-day streak. You just got real time back.

## 8. ROI beat
You took a task and cut it from its usual time to a few minutes. If it's something you do ~5×/week and you saved ~30 minutes each time, that's roughly **2.5 hours back every week** — about *ten full days a year* — from one prompt you now own.

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **Picking something too big** ("run my whole marketing"). Win small and bounded first.
- **Vague raw input.** Garbage in, garbage out — paste the actual material, not a summary of it.
- **Polishing forever.** "Usable with minor edits" is the goal today, not perfection.
- **Not saving the prompt.** The reusable prompt *is* the asset — don't lose it.

### 🔎 Honest limit
Claude works only from the input you give it; it can't see the call you didn't paste or the file you forgot to attach. And it may smooth over a detail you actually care about — so on anything client-facing, skim the result for accuracy of names, dates, numbers, and commitments before it leaves your hands.

### 📊 Case study — 40 minutes to 6, then it compounds
**Illustrative composite, consistent with reported professional use.** Maya's call-to-follow-up task (the demo above) dropped from ~40 minutes to ~6 on the first run.
