# Stop using AI like a search box — start running it like a teammate

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
You've probably typed a question into an AI, gotten a generic answer, and thought "neat, but it didn't really *do* anything for me." That's because you treated it like Google. The people getting 5–10 hours a week back treat it like a capable new hire — and that one shift is what this whole course is built on.

## 2. Concept
A search box gives you links to information. A teammate takes context, does real work, and hands you back a finished thing you can use. Claude is the second kind — but only if you brief it like you'd brief a smart new colleague: tell it the situation, what "good" looks like, and let it iterate with you.

The mental model: **you're the manager, Claude is the operator.** You decide *what* matters and judge the result; it does the drafting, sorting, analyzing, and reformatting. Where it shines: turning messy input into structured output, drafting and rewriting, summarizing long material, explaining and planning. Where it's weak (be honest): it doesn't know today's private facts about your business, it can occasionally state something wrong with confidence, and it can't take real-world actions unless you connect it to tools (later modules). Your job as the manager is to supply the context it lacks and to check its work — exactly what you'd do with a new hire in week one.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Pick one small, real thing on your plate right now. We'll prove the teammate model on it. Copy this and fill the brackets:

```
You're acting as my operations assistant at a [TYPE OF BUSINESS — e.g. 8-person dental clinic].
Context: [2–3 sentences of the real situation. Who's involved, what just happened, what I need.]
The job: [the concrete thing you want produced — a draft, a summary, a plan, a checklist].
What "good" looks like: [format, length, tone, anything that must be included].
Before you start, if anything important is missing, ask me up to 2 questions. Then do the work.
```

Send it. If it asks clarifying questions, answer them — that's the teammate working *with* you, not at you. Read the result and notice it's a finished thing, not a list of tips.

> **Privacy note:** keep out anything you wouldn't share with a new contractor (full client records, passwords, sensitive personal data). You can describe the situation without pasting the raw confidential data.

## 5. Check
Done well = (1) your prompt gave **real context** and a **clear definition of "good,"** not just a one-line question; (2) Claude produced a **finished, usable artifact** (not generic advice); (3) you can name *one thing you'd still check or correct* before using it. Your submission is auto-checked and you'll get specific feedback.

## 6. Apply to YOUR work
Now point it at the task you most want off your plate. In one message, describe that recurring task and ask: *"If you were my teammate owning this, what context would you need from me each time to do it well?"* Save its answer — that list is the seed of the workflow you'll automate later in the course.

## 7. Retain
**Quick check:** What's the one-line difference between using AI as a search box vs. a teammate? *(A search box returns information; a teammate takes context and returns finished work you can use.)* — 🔥 Day 1, mindset unlocked. Everything else builds on this.

## 8. ROI beat
You just learned the move that turns "interesting answers" into "work that's done." For a task you do ~3×/week that takes 20 minutes, briefing it as a teammate instead of redoing it yourself is roughly **45–60 minutes back every week** — before you've automated a single thing.

---

### 🎁 Keepable asset — the "Teammate Briefing" template
Save this. It's the skeleton of every good request you'll make for the rest of the course:

```
You're acting as my [ROLE] at a [BUSINESS / CONTEXT].
Context: [the real situation in 2–3 sentences]
The job: [the concrete output you want]
What "good" looks like: [format, length, tone, must-includes]
If anything important is missing, ask me up to 2 questions first, then do the work.
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **One-line questions.** "Write me an email" gives you a generic email. Context is the whole game.
- **No definition of "good."** If you don't say the format/tone/length, you'll get *a* version, not *your* version.
- **Treating its first draft as final.** A teammate iterates. "Make it shorter and warmer" takes seconds.
- **Assuming it knows your private facts.** It doesn't — feed it the specifics it needs.

### 🔎 Honest limit
Claude can sound confident even when it's wrong, and it doesn't know private or very recent facts about your business unless you tell it. Treat its output like a sharp new hire's first draft: great starting point, *you* still own the final check — especially for numbers, names, dates, and anything client-facing.
