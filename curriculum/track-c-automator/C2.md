# Cowork task loops: hand off a multi-step job and supervise, don't do

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
In your first lessons you handed Claude *one* step at a time. The leap toward **{{deliverable}}**: hand it a **whole multi-step task** and let it work the loop — gather, do, check, report — while you supervise instead of operate. This is where "AI helps me" becomes "AI does it" in {{industry}}.

## 2. Concept
Claude Cowork is the mode where Claude works **agentically** on a multi-step task: it takes an action, looks at the result, decides the next step, and repeats — a *loop* — instead of waiting for you to prompt each move. *(Cowork's exact surface and permissions evolve — check current Claude docs; the durable design below is what matters.)*

**Framework — the BRIEF→GUARDRAILS→GATES loop (safe delegation):**

1. **BRIEF** — the goal + the ordered steps (your C1 map drops straight in here) + the inputs.
2. **GUARDRAILS** — what it **MAY** do autonomously (the mechanical steps) vs. what it **MUST STOP AND ASK** before (anything irreversible: send, pay, delete, customer-facing).
3. **GATES** — the human review checkpoints, placed exactly at your C1 **[JUDGMENT]** steps. The loop runs the mechanical glue; it pauses at the judgment moments for you.
4. **REVIEW + TIGHTEN** — run on a small batch, read where it paused, refine the brief so the next run needs fewer interruptions.

The mindset shift from earlier tracks: you're no longer the operator clicking through steps — you're the **supervisor** who wrote the brief and approves the checkpoints. Start **draft-only** (it proposes, never executes) on a small batch so a mistake costs nothing, then widen the leash as it earns trust.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Use the automation target you scoped in C1. Write a Cowork BRIEF — start **draft-only**:

```
TASK GOAL: [the outcome — e.g. "triage this batch of returns and draft a response for each"]
STEPS (from my C1 map):
1. [step]  2. [step]  3. [step] …
INPUTS: [where the work/material is — paste or describe what you'll provide]
GUARDRAILS:
- You MAY: [the safe, mechanical actions — e.g. "read, classify, draft"]
- You MUST STOP AND ASK before: [irreversible — sending, refunding, deleting, customer-facing]
- Flag anything ambiguous instead of guessing.
OUTPUT: a summary table of what you did + a list of items needing my decision.
```

Run it on a **small real batch** first. Watch where it pauses, review its drafts, note any step where the brief needed to be clearer. Tighten and re-run — that iteration *is* the skill.

> **Privacy & safety note:** only let Cowork touch data/tools you're authorized to use; keep the STOP-AND-ASK gate on anything that spends money, contacts a customer, or can't be undone. Review before you widen permissions.

## 5. Check
**Done when:**
- Your brief has **explicit ordered steps** (from the C1 map).
- **Guardrails** name what it MAY do vs. what it MUST ask first.
- **Gates** sit on the irreversible/judgment steps.
- You **ran it draft-only on a real batch** and reviewed the output + escalations (didn't let it act unsupervised).

## 6. Apply to YOUR work
Run your loop on a second, slightly bigger batch. Ask Claude: *"Based on that run, rewrite my brief so the loop needs fewer interruptions — what guardrail or step should I clarify?"* Save the refined brief; it's the core of **{{deliverable}}**.

## 7. Retain
**Quick check:** In a Cowork loop, what's your job vs. Claude's? *(Claude operates the steps in a loop; you supervise — you wrote the brief and you approve the human-gated checkpoints.)* — 🔧 You just delegated a whole task, not a step.

## 8. ROI beat
Delegating a mapped multi-step task usually turns a *do-it-all* session into a *review-only* one — often an **80–90% time cut** on that task. On Priya's returns (~8 hrs/week baseline), supervising instead of operating claws back the lion's share of it.

### 🎁 Keepable asset — the "Cowork Task Brief" template
```
TASK GOAL: [outcome]
STEPS: 1… 2… 3… (from your C1 map)
INPUTS: [what you provide / where it is]
GUARDRAILS: MAY [safe actions] · MUST ASK before [irreversible actions] · flag ambiguity
GATES: pause for my approval at → [the C1 judgment steps]
OUTPUT: summary table of actions + list of items needing my decision
(Start draft-only on a small batch; widen the leash as trust grows.)
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **No STOP-AND-ASK gate.** Never let a loop send, pay, or delete unsupervised on day one.
- **A vague goal.** "Handle returns" loops aimlessly; give it the mapped steps.
- **Going full-auto immediately.** Start draft-only on a small batch; earn the autonomy.
- **Ignoring escalations.** The items it flags are exactly the ones that need your judgment — that's the system working.

### 🔎 Honest limit
An agentic loop can compound a mistake — one wrong assumption repeated across 60 items. It can also misread an ambiguous case and "confidently" proceed if you didn't tell it to stop. Cowork is powerful precisely because it acts, so the guardrails and gates aren't polish — they're the safety system. Keep irreversible actions behind a human gate until the loop has earned trust on smaller, reversible runs.

### 📊 Case study — "start simple" is the expert's advice too
**From Anthropic's engineering team.** In *Building effective agents*, Anthropic's own guidance is blunt: don't reach for an autonomous agent when a simpler, more controlled approach works — and when you do build agentic loops, design them around human oversight and clear stopping points. That's precisely this lesson's "start draft-only on a small batch, widen the leash as it earns trust." The teams that get burned by agentic AI almost always did the opposite: handed a loop full autonomy on day one with no STOP-AND-ASK gate. The BRIEF → GUARDRAILS → GATES pattern isn't caution for beginners — it's how practitioners ship agents that don't blow up.
