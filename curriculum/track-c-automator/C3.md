# Agents & subagents: split a big job into a team, not one tired prompt

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
Some jobs are too big for one prompt — you ask for everything at once and get a shallow, muddled result. To make **{{deliverable}}** robust in {{industry}}, you do what a good manager does: break the job into focused roles and let a lead coordinate them. Today you turn one overwhelmed request into a small, sharp team.

## 2. Concept
An **agent** is Claude working toward a goal with some autonomy (the Cowork loop from C2). A **subagent** is a focused helper the lead hands one slice of the job — each working in its own clean context, then reporting back to the lead that assembles the result.

**Framework — DECOMPOSE → DELEGATE → SYNTHESIZE → VERIFY:**

1. **DECOMPOSE** the deliverable into 2–4 **non-overlapping** roles, each owning one job with a clear **input → output**. (Overlap = wasted coordination; one job each.)
2. **DELEGATE** each slice to a focused subagent — focus beats a single prompt juggling five jobs, and a fresh context per role keeps a long job from drowning in its own mess.
3. **SYNTHESIZE** — the **lead** combines the subagents' outputs and adds the "so what" the parts can't see on their own. Three summaries aren't a report; the lead makes it one.
4. **VERIFY** — decomposition improves focus, not truthfulness. Each slice still gets checked (numbers, sources) before the lead stitches it in.

The mental model is an **org chart**: you brief the lead ("project manager") on the goal and the roles; it delegates, collects, and synthesizes. *(How you spin up subagents differs by surface — Claude Code and the Agent SDK expose this directly; the durable idea is "decompose into focused roles with a coordinator." Check current docs.)* Reach for this only when a job is genuinely too big for one prompt — 2–4 roles is the sweet spot; ten is harder to manage than one good prompt.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Pick a **larger, multi-part job** from your work (a report, a research+draft+review task, anything with distinct sub-jobs). Decompose it:

```
I want to run this as a LEAD agent + focused SUBAGENTS. Here's the job:
GOAL: [the single final deliverable — e.g. "a 1-page weekly performance report"]
Break it into 2–4 SUBAGENT ROLES. For each, give me:
  - role name + the ONE job it owns
  - the input it needs and the output it returns
Then describe how the LEAD combines their outputs into the final deliverable,
and one VERIFY step per role (what I should check before trusting it).
My job, roughly: [DESCRIBE the big task and its parts].
```

Then actually run one or two of the subagent jobs (paste their slice of input, get the focused output) and have Claude assemble them. Notice each piece is sharper than a single mega-prompt would produce.

> **Privacy note:** give each subagent only the data its slice needs — don't hand the whole dataset to a role that only summarizes one part of it.

## 5. Check
**Done when:**
- The job is **decomposed into 2–4 non-overlapping roles**, each with a clear input → output.
- There's a **lead/synthesis step** that combines the parts into one deliverable (not just loose summaries).
- You've named a **verify step** per role.
- You **ran at least the decomposition** and the result is more focused than a single prompt.

## 6. Apply to YOUR work
Take your capstone workflow and ask: *"Which parts of this are distinct enough to be subagents, and what's each one's single job?"* Saving that role breakdown turns a fragile mega-prompt into a maintainable little team — and sets up the scheduled, hands-off version of **{{deliverable}}** in C4.

## 7. Retain
**Quick check:** Why split a big job into subagents instead of one prompt — and what does the lead add? *(Focus + clean context per role; the lead synthesizes the parts and adds the "so what" no single role can see.)* — 🔧 You can now run a job too big for a single prompt.

## 8. ROI beat
Decomposition turns "I can't trust the AI with the whole report" into "I review three tight pieces and a synthesis." On a multi-source weekly deliverable that's commonly **1.5–2.5 hours back per week**, with noticeably higher quality because each part was done with focus.

### 🎁 Keepable asset — the "Decompose into Subagents" template
```
GOAL: [the single final deliverable]
ROLES (2–4, non-overlapping):
  • [name] — owns [one job] — input: [...] → output: [...]
  • [name] — owns [one job] — input: [...] → output: [...]
LEAD: how it combines the outputs into the final deliverable + the "so what"
VERIFY: per role, what I check before trusting it (numbers · sources · scope)
My job: [DESCRIBE]
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **Roles that overlap.** Two subagents doing similar work add coordination cost for nothing. One job each.
- **Too many subagents.** 2–4 is usually right; ten roles is harder to manage than one good prompt.
- **No synthesis step.** Three summaries aren't a report — the lead must combine and add the "so what."
- **Over-engineering small jobs.** If one focused prompt does it, use one prompt. Subagents are for genuinely big, multi-part work.

### 🔎 Honest limit
More agents means more places for an error to enter and more to supervise — a subagent can confidently return a flawed slice, and the lead will faithfully stitch it in. Decomposition improves focus, not truthfulness: verify each part (especially numbers and sources) before trusting the assembled whole. Reach for subagents when the job is truly too big for one prompt, not by default.

### 📊 Case study — Anthropic runs its own research this way
**A real production system.** Anthropic published how it built the multi-agent research feature behind Claude: a **lead agent** that plans and coordinates, spawning **focused subagents** that each investigate one slice in their own context, then report back for the lead to synthesize. The team found this orchestrator-plus-subagents shape outperformed a single agent on complex, breadth-heavy research — for the exact reason this lesson gives: focus and a clean context per role beat one prompt juggling everything. They also flagged the catch you'll recognize: more agents means more cost and more places for error, so you decompose only when the job genuinely warrants it. That's DECOMPOSE → DELEGATE → SYNTHESIZE → VERIFY, in production.
