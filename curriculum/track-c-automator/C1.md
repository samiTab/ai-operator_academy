# Map a workflow worth automating (before you automate the wrong thing)

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
The fastest way to waste a weekend is to automate something that didn't matter — or to automate it before you understood it. This is the first step toward **{{deliverable}}**: today you pick the *right* workflow in {{industry}}, map it into clear steps, and measure what it actually costs you, so everything you build next genuinely pays off.

## 2. Concept
Automation is just a workflow you've understood well enough to delegate. So the real work happens *before* any tool — and it follows a repeatable framework.

**Framework — MAP → MARK → MEASURE → TARGET (the automation triage):**

1. **MAP** the workflow as discrete steps, each with an **input → action → output**. If you can't write the steps down, you can't automate them.
2. **MARK** each step as **[MECHANICAL]** (rule-based, predictable — safe to hand off) or **[JUDGMENT]** (needs a human's call — keep it). Most workflows are ~70% mechanical glue around a few judgment moments.
3. **MEASURE** the baseline: *frequency × minutes-per-run = time/week and time/month.* This single number decides whether a build is worth it and becomes your ROI proof.
4. **TARGET** the run of mechanical steps with the highest (frequency × time) — that's where you automate first.

The reason most automation projects fail isn't technical — it's that people automate a rare task, or skip the baseline and can't prove value, or try to automate a judgment call. The framework prevents all three. Claude's role here is **interviewer**: it pulls the steps out of your head faster than a blank page and spots the automatable seams.

```
   1) … 2) … 3) …
2. MARK    — tag each step [MECHANICAL] or [JUDGMENT]
3. MEASURE — runs/period × minutes each = time/week + time/month   (BASELINE)
4. TARGET  — best 2–4 contiguous MECHANICAL steps (highest freq × time) + why
AUTOMATION BRIEF (one line):
  "Automate [steps X–Y] of [workflow], ~[N] hrs/month, keeping [judgment] human."
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **Automating a rare task.** A 2×/year process isn't worth a build. Chase frequency × time.
- **Skipping the baseline.** No number = no way to prove ROI or prioritize. MEASURE is non-negotiable.
- **Automating judgment steps.** Approvals, exceptions, and relationship calls stay human (for now).
- **Mapping the ideal process, not the real one.** Map what *actually* happens — mess, exceptions, and all.

### 🔎 Honest limit
Claude maps the workflow *you describe* — if your mental model skips a step (the silent exception you handle on autopilot), the map will too, and an automation built on it will choke on that case. Walk one or two *real recent instances* against the map to catch the steps you forgot you do.

### 📊 Case study — Drucker's warning, 60 years early
**A genuinely verifiable quote.** Management thinker **Peter Drucker** wrote: *"There is nothing so useless as doing efficiently that which should not be done at all."* That sentence is the entire MEASURE → TARGET step. The most common automation failure isn't technical — it's automating a task that was rare, or low-value, or shouldn't exist. A real pattern from operations teams: someone spends a weekend automating a report nobody reads, feels productive, and saves zero real hours. The baseline number (frequency × time) is what protects you from efficient uselessness — it forces the question *should this run at all* before *how do I make it run.*
