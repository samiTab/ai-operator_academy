# Governance, safety & measurement: guardrails that protect without strangling

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
The fastest way to lose AI at your company in {{industry}} is a preventable incident — a leaked client file, a wrong number sent to a customer, a "10x productivity" claim that doesn't survive scrutiny. Today you put lightweight guardrails in place so your AI program is *trusted*: safe with data, sane on review, and honest about ROI — and those guardrails are what make **{{deliverable}}** sustainable, not just a launch.

## 2. Concept
Governance for a small team isn't a 40-page policy — it's a **one-page set of rules everyone actually follows**, covering three areas. **Data handling:** what's safe to put into AI and what's not (client PII, contracts, credentials, regulated data), where company data goes, and which tools/connectors are approved. **Review checkpoints:** which AI outputs need a human sign-off before they go out (customer-facing, financial, legal, irreversible) — this is the team-scale version of the gates you've used all course. **Honest measurement:** track real ROI (time saved, quality, error rates) *truthfully*, including where AI *didn't* help, so decisions and claims are credible.

**Framework — DATA → GATE → TRACK → TRUST (the governance engine):**

1. **DATA:** Define what's safe vs. never-put-in-AI, which tools and connectors are approved, and where company data goes. Name the categories explicitly: client PII, contracts, credentials, regulated data, proprietary info. If it's not on the "safe" list, it's on the "never" list.
2. **GATE:** Map sensitive output types to a named human approver before they go out. Customer-facing, financial, legal, and irreversible outputs need a gate. "Should be reviewed" is not a gate. "Nadia signs off before any client-facing brief leaves the system" is.
3. **TRACK:** Measure real ROI honestly — time saved, quality changes, error rates — and include a "where it didn't help" column. Vanity metrics (only the wins) destroy credibility over time. Honest tracking is what keeps leadership saying yes and the team trusting the data.
4. **TRUST:** Share the results — including the misses — with the team and with leadership on a regular cadence. Proof builds credibility; transparency builds trust. A program that survives is one that earns repeated consent, not one that hides its shortcomings.

The mindset: governance is what lets you *say yes* to more AI, not a brake. Clear rules remove the fear that makes leaders ban tools outright.

Client data is heavily regulated (GDPR, fiduciary obligations), and his team is nervous about using AI at all. He applies DATA→GATE→TRACK→TRUST: DATA ("only use anonymized case scenarios — never paste client names, account numbers, or portfolio details into any external AI; the only approved tool is the firm's contracted workspace with a data processing agreement in place"); GATE ("any output that references specific portfolio recommendations or client communication must be reviewed by Ben before sending — no exceptions"); TRACK ("quarterly review: hours saved on research and report drafting, error rate on first-draft reports, and at least one honest 'where AI produced a wrong answer'"); TRUST (he shares the quarterly review with clients in the firm's transparency report, turning governance into a market differentiator). **Team AI anxiety: high → managed. TEAM AI RULES (v1 · owner: [role] · review: quarterly)

```
1. DATA:   SAFE to use AI for […]
           NEVER put in AI: [client PII · contracts · credentials · regulated data]
           Approved tools: […] · data stays in: […]

           [customer-facing] → [approver role]
           [financial/legal] → [approver role]
           [irreversible]    → [approver role]

3. TRACK:  Monthly — hours saved · quality score · error rate · WHERE AI DIDN'T HELP (be honest)

4. TRUST:  Share results at [monthly team meeting / quarterly leadership report]
           Include the misses — credibility comes from transparency

Compliance note: [industry-specific — verify with expert where flagged]
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **A policy too long to read.** If it's not one page, no one follows it. Practical beats comprehensive.
- **No data line on regulated info.** The biggest risks are PII, contracts, and regulated data — name them.
- **Vanity metrics.** Reporting only wins destroys credibility; log the misses too.
- **Checkpoints with no named owner.** "Should be reviewed" ≠ "X signs off." Name the human.

### 🔎 Honest limit
This is a *starting framework*, not legal/compliance advice — regulated industries (health, finance, legal, anything with personal data under laws like GDPR) have specific obligations you must verify with a qualified expert. Governance also can't catch every misuse; it reduces risk and builds trust, but it pairs with the human review gates and conservative scoping you've used throughout. When the stakes or the data are sensitive, slow down and get expert sign-off.

### 📊 Case study — your one-pager mirrors the national standard
**Grounded in a real framework.** In 2023 the U.S. **National Institute of Standards and Technology** released the **AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)**, organized around four functions: **Govern, Map, Measure, Manage.** Your DATA → GATE → TRACK → TRUST sheet is the small-team version of exactly that: govern what data goes where, map and gate the risky outputs, measure honestly, and manage trust through transparency. The point isn't bureaucracy — NIST's own framing is that good AI governance *enables* responsible use rather than blocking it. For regulated work, this is also where the **EU AI Act** (entering force from 2024) and sector rules like GDPR or HIPAA apply — which is why this lesson's honest limit insists: when the data is sensitive, verify with a qualified expert.
