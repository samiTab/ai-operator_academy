# Rollout & adoption: the tool isn't the hard part — the people are

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
Most AI rollouts fail the same way in {{industry}}: leadership buys licenses, sends a link, and three weeks later nobody's using it. You designed a role (D1) and built shared Skills (D2) — today you plan the rollout so your team *actually adopts* them, turning **{{deliverable}}** from a design document into a running system.

## 2. Concept
Adoption is change management, not distribution. People resist new tools for three predictable reasons: **fear** ("will this replace me?"), **friction** ("it's faster to do it the old way"), and **doubt** ("I tried it once, it was wrong"). A rollout that ignores these dies. One that addresses them head-on wins.

**Framework — PILOT → WIN → CHAMPION → MEASURE (the adoption engine):**

1. **PILOT:** Start with one small willing group — not the whole team. One process, one department, one week. A pilot fails safely and teaches; a big-bang rollout fails visibly and permanently poisons the well.
2. **WIN:** Design a concrete hands-on first win in the pilot's first session. Not a demo — everyone *does the thing* on a real work task and finishes it faster. The first win is the evidence that converts skeptics: they felt it, not just heard about it.
3. **CHAMPION:** Identify the 1–2 most enthusiastic pilot users and make them peer advocates. People trust colleagues more than mandates. Champions answer questions, share tips, and create the social proof that pulls others in.
4. **MEASURE:** Track 2–3 process metrics (time saved, quality, error rate — not individual surveillance) and share the results explicitly. Proof pulls wider adoption far better than mandate. If results are underwhelming, that's feedback — fix D1/D2 before expanding.

Address the fear **directly and honestly**: frame AI as removing the tedious parts of jobs, be straight about what's changing, and never pretend. Trust is the real adoption currency.

## 3. Demo
**Nadia rolls out the Intake Coordinator role** to her 25-person agency.

1. **PILOT:** she starts with the 4-person account team, not everyone. ![demo](screenshot: a rollout plan showing a 4-person pilot before company-wide)
2. **WIN + CHAMPION:** a 30-min session where each person uses the brief Skill on a real request; two enthusiastic users become champions. ![demo](screenshot: a hands-on session with two people tagged as champions)
3. **FEAR:** she's explicit — *"this kills the brief-formatting grind, not your job; here's what you'll do with the freed time."* ![demo](screenshot: an honest FAQ slide addressing 'will this replace me')
4. **MEASURE:** after 2 weeks, pilot saved ~5 hrs/week with better briefs; she shares the numbers, then expands. ![demo](screenshot: a results recap with hours saved, shared before the wider rollout)

**Second example — healthcare clinic, front-desk AI rollout.** Marcus is the clinic director rolling AI-assisted appointment intake to 12 front-desk staff across 3 locations. He applies PILOT→WIN→CHAMPION→MEASURE: PILOT (one location, 4 staff — the most change-friendly location; 1 week); WIN (in the first training session, each staff member uses the intake Skill to process one real walk-in — they finish 3 minutes faster than usual; that's the win they feel); CHAMPION (the receptionist who was most sceptical but most impressed becomes the peer advocate — her endorsement carries far more weight than Marcus's); MEASURE (tracks average intake time and intake form completeness rate over 2 weeks, shares the numbers at the all-staff meeting before rolling to the other two locations). The whole-clinic rollout takes 6 weeks instead of a single mandate that produces 6 weeks of workarounds. The PILOT→WIN→CHAMPION→MEASURE loop works in healthcare, trades, agencies, and any sector where people are the variable.

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **Big-bang rollout.** Company-wide on day one means company-wide failure. Pilot first.
- **Mandate without proof.** "You must use this" breeds resentment; proof pulls adoption.
- **Dodging the fear.** Vague reassurance reads as a threat. Be specific and honest.
- **Surveillance metrics.** Tracking individuals kills trust; measure the process outcome.

### 🔎 Honest limit
You can't make people trust a tool that's actually unreliable — if the role/Skill produces bad output, adoption *should* fail, and no rollout polish will (or should) save it. Rollout amplifies whatever you built: make sure D1/D2 are genuinely solid first, be honest about limitations, and let real results — not pressure — do the convincing. Forced adoption of a weak tool is worse than no adoption.
