# Design an AI role: write the job description before you hire the AI

## 1. Hook (≈15s read)
The teams that win with AI in {{industry}} don't "let everyone use it." They define **roles** — "the AI does X, owns this lane, hands off here." Today you write a real job description for an AI teammate, the same way you'd scope a new hire, so its work is consistent, safe, and actually adopted — and that designed role becomes the foundation of **{{deliverable}}**.

## 2. Concept
A vague "use AI for support" produces chaos — everyone prompts differently and quality scatters. A **defined AI role** fixes the lane: what it owns, what it must escalate to a human, what "good" output looks like, and where it hands off. It's a job description, not a prompt. (Notice this is the team-scale version of the F1 "teammate" idea: instead of *you* briefing it ad hoc, you write the role *once* for everyone.)

**Framework — MISSION → SCOPE → ESCALATE → DONE (the AI role design spec):**

1. **MISSION:** One sentence: the outcome this role is accountable for. Not the tasks — the *outcome*. "Turn raw client requests into structured briefs" is a mission. "Help with intake" is not.
2. **SCOPE:** What it does — and an explicit "does NOT do" list. Undefined scope is how an AI role oversteps into pricing, promises, or sensitive decisions it should escalate. Both the "does" and "does NOT do" sides are mandatory.
3. **ESCALATE:** The specific situations where it must stop and hand to a named human. "Escalate if unsure" is useless — name the situations and the owner. Budget questions → account lead. Complaints → customer success lead. This is what makes the role safe.
4. **DONE:** The quality bar for good output — a checklist or template a reviewer could use to confirm the output is complete. Without this, "done" is whatever the AI decides, and that varies.

Scope and Escalation are where most teams fail. An AI role without clear boundaries either overreaches or gets ignored.

**Second example — law firm, AI research assistant.** David is a partner at a boutique litigation firm where junior associates spend 40% of their time on routine case-law research. He designs the MISSION→SCOPE→ESCALATE→DONE framework: MISSION ("produce a sourced case-law summary on a defined legal question"); SCOPE ("does: search, summarize, cite sources — does NOT: render legal advice, assess strategy, or make admissibility judgments"); ESCALATE ("any question that touches client strategy or liability exposure → escalate to the supervising associate before the memo leaves the Skill"); DONE ("a structured memo: question, holdings summary, key cases with citations, open questions"). The role spec lets the associates delegate the research leg while keeping the judgment calls in human hands. **~3 hrs of research/associate/week → ~45 min review.** The MISSION→SCOPE→ESCALATE→DONE framework is the same in professional services, healthcare admin, or any other field — only the domain content changes.

## 4. Do it (the core — hands-on)
Pick one team function in {{industry}} you'd like an AI teammate to own. Write its role with Claude:

```
Help me write a job description for an AI teammate.
ROLE NAME: [e.g. "Intake Coordinator"]
Draft these four sections, and pressure-test my boundaries:
1. MISSION: the outcome it's accountable for (one sentence — outcome, not tasks).
2. SCOPE: what it DOES — and an explicit "does NOT do" list (both sides required).
3. ESCALATE: the situations where it must STOP and hand to a named human (be specific — name the situation AND the owner).
4. DONE: the quality bar — a checklist a reviewer could use to confirm output is complete.
Context: [your team, the function, what currently goes wrong].
Challenge me on anything under-scoped or any missing escalation.
```

The deliverable is a **one-page AI role** clear enough that a teammate could read it and know exactly what the AI handles vs. what stays human.

> **Privacy note:** name the escalation owners by role, not personal data; keep client specifics generic in the role doc itself.

## 8. ROI beat
A defined role replaces scattered, inconsistent AI use with one reliable function. For an intake/triage role handling, say, 40 requests/week, structured handling instead of ad-hoc commonly saves the team **3–6 hours/week** *and* cuts the rework from inconsistent output.

### 🎁 Keepable asset — the "AI Role Description" template
```
ROLE: [name]
MISSION:   the outcome it owns (1 sentence — outcome, not tasks)
SCOPE:     does […] · does NOT do […]  (both sides required)
ESCALATE:  [specific situation] → hand to [named human role]  (list each)
DONE:      the quality bar — checklist a reviewer uses to confirm output is complete
```

### ⚠️ Common mistakes
- **No "does NOT do" list.** Undefined scope is how an AI role oversteps into pricing, promises, or HR.
- **Vague escalation.** "Escalate if unsure" is useless — name the *situations* and the *owner*.
- **Designing for the tool, not the outcome.** Start from the mission, not "where can we use AI."
- **Too broad a role.** "AI does marketing" isn't a role. One function, clear lane.

### 🔎 Honest limit
A role description guides behavior but doesn't *enforce* it — the AI can still misjudge an edge case or an employee can use it outside its lane. The role is the foundation; the Skills (D2), human escalation gates, and governance (D4) are what make it dependable. Design conservatively: when in doubt, put it in the escalation column, not the scope column.

### 📊 Case study — why most AI initiatives stall (it isn't the model)
**A well-documented pattern.** Across studies of enterprise AI adoption — including MIT Sloan Management Review / BCG's long-running research — the consistent finding is that AI initiatives fail far more often on *organizational* factors (unclear scope, no process ownership, weak adoption) than on model capability. A capable model pointed at an undefined job produces inconsistent, unsafe, or ignored output. That's the entire case for MISSION → SCOPE → ESCALATE → DONE: writing the role like a job description — especially the "does NOT do" list and named escalation owners — converts "we use AI for support" (chaos) into a function with a lane, the single biggest predictor of whether the deployment survives contact with a real team.

Define the role like a job description, not a prompt — a mission, a lane, and clear handoffs.
